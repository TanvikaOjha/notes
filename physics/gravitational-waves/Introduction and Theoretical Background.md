**_1.1 GENERAL THEORY OF RELATIVITY:_**

**_GENERAL THEORY OF RELATIVITY also referred to as Einstein’s theory of gravity, is the geometric theory of gravitation published by Albert Einstein in May 1916 which provides a unified description of gravity as a geometric property of the four-dimensional spacetime._**

**_The curvature of spacetime is directly related to the energy, momentum and stress of whatever is present, including matter and radiation. This relation is specified by the Einstein field equation:_**

                Gμν+Λgμν=κTμν,**_![](file:///C:/Users/Samsung/AppData/Local/Temp/msohtmlclip1/01/clip_image004.jpg)_**

**_This theory has astrophysical implications, including the prediction of black holes- regions of space in which space and time are distorted in such a way that nothing, not even light can escape from them. It also predicts gravitational lensing, where the bending of light results in distorted and multiple images of the same distant astronomical phenomenon. One of the most important predictions also include the existence of gravitational waves._**

**_1.2 GRAVITATIONAL WAVES_****_:_** **HOW ACCELERATING MASSIVE OBJECTS RIPPLE SPACE TIME**

**_Gravitational waves are ‘ripples’ in space-time caused by some of the most violent and energetic processes in the Universe. Massive accelerating objects (things like neutron stars or black holes orbiting each other) disrupt space-time in such a way that waves of undulating space-time propagate in all direction away from the source. These waves travel with the speed of light, carrying with them information about their origins, as well as clues to the nature of gravity itself._**

**_The strongest gravitational waves are produced by cataclysmic events such as colliding black holes, supernovae (massive stars exploding at the end of their lifetime), and colliding neutron stars. Other predicted sources include rotation of neutron stars and the remnants of gravitational radiation created by big bang._**

**_1.4 SOURCES AND TYPES OF GRAVITATIONAL WAVES_**

**_Astronomers have defined four categories of gravitational waves based on what object or system generates the waves:_**

·       **_Compact Binary Inspiral Gravitational Waves_**

·       **_Continuous Gravitational Waves_**

·       **_Stochastic Gravitational Waves_**

·       **_Burst Gravitational Waves_**

**_1.4.1 Compact Binary Inspiral Gravitational Waves:_**

**_These waves are produced by orbiting pairs of massive and dense(“compact”) objects like blackholes and neutron stars. There are three subclasses of ‘compact binary’ systems in this category:_**

**_1.Binary Neutron Stars (BNS): two neutron stars orbiting each other._**

**_2.Binary Black Hole (BBH): two black holes orbiting each other_**

**_3.Neutron Star-Black Hole Binary (NSBH): a neutron star and a black hole orbiting each other._**


**_Each binary pair creates a unique pattern of gravitational waves but the mechanism of wave generation is the same across all three-Inspiral. Inspiral occurs over millions of years as pairs of dense compact objects revolve around each other. As they orbit, they radiate gravitational waves that carry away some of the system’s orbital energy. Over millennia, this causes the objects to move closer and closer together. The closer they are, the faster they orbit each other, which causes them to radiate stronger gravitational waves, which causes them to lose more orbital energy, inch ever closer, orbit faster, lose more energy, move closer, orbit faster… etc. The objects are doomed, inescapably locked in a runaway accelerating spiraling embrace. The process of emitting gravitational waves and orbiting closer and closer sets off an unstoppable sequence of events that can only end with the two objects colliding._**

**_1.4.2 Continuous Gravitational Waves:_**

**_These waves are expected to be produced by a single spinning massive object like a neutron star. Any bumps on or imperfections in the spherical shape of this star will generate gravitational waves as it spins. If the spin-rate of the star stays constant, so too will the gravitational waves it emits. That is, the gravitational wave is continuously of the same frequency and amplitude. That’s why these are called ‘Continuous Gravitational Waves’._**

**_1.4.3 Stochastic Gravitational Waves_**

**_Astronomers presume that many small gravitational waves are passing by from all over the Universe all the time, and that they are mixed together at random. These small waves from every direction make up what is called “Stochastic Signal”, so called because the word ‘stochastic’ means having a random pattern that may be analyzed statistically but not predicted precisely. These will be the smallest and most difficult gravitational waves to detect, but it is possible that at least part of this stochastic signal may originate from the Big Bang._**

**_1.4.4 Burst Gravitational Waves_**

**_These waves come from short-duration unknown or unanticipated sources. Searching for these kinds of signals requires being utterly open-minded. If you don’t know what you are looking for, it’s really hard to find it! While this makes searching for burst gravitational Waves difficult, detecting them has the greatest potential to reveal revolutionary information about the Universe._**

**_1.5 CHIRPS_**

**_A gravitational wave “chirp” is the characteristic strain signal generated by the inspiral, merger, and ringdown of compact binary systems. The morphology is defined by a distinct waveform that continuously increases in both frequency and amplitude over time until a sudden peak, followed by a rapid decay._**

**The diagram represents the event GW150914, which is attributed to a merger of two black holes. The bottom graph shows two curves. The black curve, trending downward, represents the separation between the objects. The green curve, trending upward, represents the objects’ increasing orbital speed as they spiral inward.**

**_The morphology is broken down into three distinct, mathematically modeled phases:_**

**_1.5.1 The Inspiral Phase_**

**_As two compact objects orbit one another, they lose energy by radiating gravitational waves. This causes their orbits to shrink, which forces the objects to rotate faster. This creates a slowly oscillating wave that gradually increases in frequency and amplitude. The rate at which frequency sweeps upward is dictated by the system’s chirp mass (M),  a specific combination of individual masses (m1 and m2):_**

**_Because this mass dominates the phase evolution it is most accurately measured parameter in gravitational wave astronomy._**

**_1.5.2 The Merger Phase_**

**_The two bodies reach their closest stable orbit and plunge into each other to form single, highly distorted object. This represents the cataclysmic climax of the chirp. The frequency and amplitude peak sharply and then reach an abrupt cutoff (often referred to as the ‘trough’ of the chirp) as the horizon settles._**

**_1.5.3 The Ringdown Phase_**

**_The newly formed, single blackhole is initially highly deformed. To achieve a stable, symmetrical state, it vibrates and sheds its asymmetry by radiating gravitational waves. The signal rapidly decays in a series of damped, exponentially fading oscillations called quasi-normal mode._**
